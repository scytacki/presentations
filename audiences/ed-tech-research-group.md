# Education Technology Research Group

## Overview

A medium-sized (10-25 people) education technology research group with a mix of roles: developers, project managers, researchers, and principal investigators. Everyone has at least spreadsheet-level technical comfort, and most are already using AI tools in some capacity.

## Roles

### Principal Investigators (PIs)
- Write proposals to secure grant funding, usually from the NSF
- Oversee the work of project managers, researchers, and developers to implement the grant

### Project Managers
- Track what everyone is doing
- Help the PI keep the project on track

### Researchers
- Design evaluation methods for the focus of the proposal
- Review data collected from classroom testing
- Data sources include:
  - Log messages from the software
  - Student answers to questions
  - Software state changes recorded during use
  - Classroom observations
  - Audio and video recordings of classrooms
  - Student interviews
- Research often covers both students and teachers

### Developers
- Create the software that will be tested in classrooms

## How Projects Work

1. A PI writes a proposal with a research question and secures grant funding (typically NSF)
2. The team builds something — either brand new or modifying something existing
3. The thing is tested in real classrooms with real students and teachers
4. The built artifact is used to answer the research question from the proposal
5. Researchers collect and analyze data from the classroom testing
6. Reports and dashboards are often provided to teachers so they can see student progress

## AI Adoption Mindsets

People in the group have different relationships with AI. These aren't levels on a ladder — they reflect where someone's focus and experience has taken them so far.

### AI as a productivity tool
People who use AI regularly and have integrated it into their core workflow. They're comfortable giving it open-ended tasks and iterating on results. Most developers fall here.

### AI as a specific assistant
People who use AI for tasks they already understand how to do. They give it concrete instructions and get concrete results. Many in this group haven't yet experienced telling AI *what* they want rather than *how* to do it — which could open up tasks they wouldn't have attempted on their own.

### AI as someone else's tool
People who see AI as valuable but haven't found a fit for their own work yet. This might be because they have other priorities, or because they haven't seen an example that connects to what they do day-to-day.

## Presentation Considerations

- The primary presentation audience is PIs, PMs, and researchers — developers are reached through regular collaboration
- People in the group have different relationships with AI, so presentations work best when they don't assume a particular level of AI experience
- The group's work is grounded in real-world outcomes — building things for classrooms, testing with students and teachers, collecting and analyzing data. Presentations that connect to this concrete, applied work will resonate more than abstract discussion of AI capabilities
